Social Cooking App

Goal: Design an app for organizing cooking events.

About the Project

My first exposure to designing user interfaces happened in college when my team and I designed a tablet app that helps people organize cooking events.

Pain Points

Through interviews, we identified two main pain points.

  1. People struggle to find time when everyone is available
  2. Emails and texts do not summarize the key details, so it’s easy to lose track of them.

My Role

In addition to contributing to research and synthesis, I helped program the prototype in HTML/CSS.

Persona Creation

We started by learning about the role of food in their lives and when/why they cook.

Two personas emerged: Corinne and Abby.

Corinne

She cooks daily, plans out what to cook each week.

Abby

Abby generally eats at the cafeteria, but would like to cook more because it's fun.

User Scenarios

Next, we illustrated the steps that our personas go through as they plan cooking events.

Corinne

Corinne is organized and clear about what she wants.

Abby

Abby sees cooking as a social experience. Unfortunately her schedule is pretty hectic, so she has difficulty finding time.

Task-Flow Analysis

For each persona, we mapped out the process by which they decide on recipes and make the food.

Abby gets stuck figuring out what to make, and Corinne often finds herself responsible for logistics.

Initial Ideation and Divergence

My team started out with sketches and shared our different solutions to inspire each other.

Paper Mockup

We then tested ideas and combined the best ones.

Overall Organization

On the left of the homepage are buttons for creating events and matching with other users. On the right is the timeline, list of friends, and recipe collection.

User Testing

TThroughout our design process, we ran usability studies. For example, we learned that the horizontal timeline didn’t have the affordance of clicking to reveal the details. To fix this, we changed to a vertical timeline where details are always shown.

Final Class Deliverable: Click-Through Mockup

At the end of the class, my team mates and I produced a click-through mockup. I helped write the HTML and CSS for the application.

Higher-Fidelity Mockup

Looking back on this project, I saw many ways that the UI could be improved. This new version allocates more screen real estate to upcoming events, the primary need identified.

I created this higher fidelity mockup in Sketch, and the click-through mockup is available through InVision.

Collaboration App for Google Docs

Goal: Make it easier to receive writing feedback through bite-sized chunks.

About the Project

One of my favorite collaboration tools is GoogleDocs, but it's limited to use on a desktop. In this project, I explored how a mobile version of GoogleDocs could improve the overall user experience. In the end, I arrived at some wireframes for a Google Docs Mobile App that focuses on "chunking" text into easily-editable chunks.

What is Google Docs?

Google Docs is a web-based word-processing tool that allows many people to simultaneously contribute to the same document. This is great for teams that are writing together.

Simplifying Functionality for Mobile

Google docs already has a mobile version, but it has some problems: navigation, focus of action, and overall ease-of-use.

Receiving Feedback is a Slow Process

Looking at the process of editing a word document on Google Docs, a pain point becomes clear: receiving edits takes time. This is because even if a writer wants feedback on a tiny change, the writer needs to send their entire draft over. Editing an entire draft requires setting aside a large chunk of time, and large chunks of time are difficult to find in a busy schedule.

A whole draft is sent.

Setting aside a couple hours to edit is difficult.

Process with a Mobile App.

What if instead, a writer could send edits over in chunks that could easily fit into the editor’s busy schedule? This would be valuable for edits around sentence flow, grammar, and sentence clarity.

The mobile platform allows for editing all the time. The advisor/editor can edit on the train or while waiting for a pot to boil. Time that would normally be idle can now be used more productively.

Primary Feature

The process would start after the writer edits a piece of writing. The writer selects it to be shared as a "chunk." This "chunk" then shows up on the mobile app for reviewers to see. All changes and comments are tracked, and show up in the desktop version of Google docs.

The opening page is where all "chunks" can be accessed. When a chunk is selected it can be edited.

Writers also need feedback on the use of figures and images, so built into the app is the ability to view figures and their associated captions.

Navigation

The home page is where an editor can keep track of the “chunks” needing feedback.

Alignment with Users' Values

Users value efficiency, productivity, and learning. This app embodies these values by making feedback easier to receive.

Future Work

The main criticism I have heard is that users might lose a sense of context when making edits. However, to keep future product development focused, it's important to remember that the goal of this app is not to replace the desktop version, but support it. It's for those times between first and final drafts when you need quick, and regular feedback.

Sustainable Cooking App

Goal: Help people cook more sustainable dinners through better choices.

Out of the Classroom

In a sustainable design course, my team and I found that the majority of impact in a meal comes from the food, not the energy used to cook it. With the goal of changing what people cook, my team came up with the idea of an app that substitutes ingredients in recipes to make them more sustainable. After the class ended, I turned the basic concepts into a vision prototype that could be tested.

Remixipe Task-Flow Analysis

Before designing the app, I analyzed the process using a flow diagram. By creating this diagram, I saw how different actions connected in the overall user journey. This helped me to organize the UI.

Low-Fidelity Mockups

Following the task flow, I sketched mockups on paper and Balsamiq. Working at such low fidelity helped me explore ideas and cover all user actions. In this current iteration, I organized the app into four main steps: Input, Remix, Cook and Review.

Input

People store and find recipes in a variety of ways, so the Input pages were designed to allow users to input recipes from these different sources.

Remix

People value different things in their meals, and the Remix page was designed to accommodate the values shared by Grant and Patricia. Users can choose between remixing their meal based on price, environmental impact, healthfulness, or preparation time.

Cook

The Cook Page allows users both do three things: Cook the recipe, save the recipe for later, or reject the current recipe to try a different remix.

Review

The review page allows users to give feedback on the flavor balance of the meal and rate it.

Future Work

Now that I have mockups that capture the basic activities, the next step is to test with users.

More specifically, I'd like to test whether or not people are interested in trying "remixed" recipes. We could start by "remixing" common recipes and see if people actually cook with the new recipes. After that, we could "remix" recipes that are user-submitted and gradually increase how much we simulate the experience of the app.

After this basic validation, then we can move onto usability of the app.
